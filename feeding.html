Wet Food vs. Dry Food

The great debate. Both wet and dry food have their pros and cons, and the best choice for your cat will depend on their individual needs and preferences. Wet food is generally more palatable and can help your cat stay hydrated, but it can be more expensive and has a shorter shelf life once opened. Dry food is more convenient, but it can be less appealing to some cats and may not provide as much moisture. Cats tend to have a pretty low thirst drive, so it's important to make sure they are getting enough water, especially if they are eating dry food. You can also try combination feeding (wet and dry) to give your cat the best of both worlds!

Understanding Ingredients

One thing I can't stress enough is the importance of protein. Cat's are what you call obligate carnivores, which means that they require a diet that is high in animal protein to thrive. When looking at a cat food label, the first ingredient should always be some form of meat (chicken, beef, fish, etc.). I will say, certain meats are better tha others. For example, chicken and turkey are generally considered to be better sources of protein than beef or certain fish. Beef tends to be very rich and be hard for cats to digest, and certain fish (like tuna) can be high in mercury. This does not mean you have to completely stay away from these ingredients, but if you plan on feeding them, it'd be best to rotate them out with other, more easily digestible proteins. Before you go onto the next sentence, I am not a vet, nor do I have a degree in feline nutrition so please do your own research outside of this site and consult with your vet before making any major changes to your cat's diet. This might get some mixed opinions, but I personally try to avoid feeding my cat foods that contain a lot of plant-based ingredients (like corn, wheat, soy, etc.) as these are not natural components of a cat's diet and can be harder for them to digest. Again, this is just my personal preference and there are many cats that do just fine on diets that include these ingredients. The most important thing is to find something that your cat enjoys and meets their nutritional needs. "Fed is best" is a common phrase in the pet community, and it really is true.


One ingredient I will say you want to look for is taurine. Taurine is an essential amino acid that cats cannot produce on their own, so it must be include in their diet. It's important for maintaining healthy vision, heart functins, and reproductive health. You can find taurine in animal-based ingredients like meat and fish, but it's also commonly added to commercial cat foods as a supplement.


Note: If you cat has a food allergy or sensitivity, it's important to check the ingredient list carefully as some foods might be labeled as "Turkey Flavor" but actually contain chicken as one of the ingredients. If your cat has a food allergy, it's best to consult with your vet to determine the best course of action and to find a diet that works for them.

Toppers & Treats

Toppers and treats can be a great way to add some variety to your cat's diet. There are a couple different options you can choose from: liquid toppers, whole food toppers, and treats. Liquid toppers are usually a broth or gravy that you can pour over your cat's food to make it more enticing. This can include items like bone broth, BUT not the human kind of bone broth that contains onions and garlic, which are toxic to cats. Whole food toppers are usually small pieces of cooked meat or fish that you can mix in with your cat's food. You can also do raw meat organ meat like chicken hearts or liver, but again, please do your research and consult with your vet before feeding your cat a raw diet. You shouldn't just buy raw meat from the grocery store as grocery store meat can be contaminated with bacteria that can be harmful to your cat.


There are also other, more health focused toppers such as pumpkin puree which is a great source of fiber and can help with digestive issues, or fish oil which is a great source of omega-3 fatty acids that can help with skin and coat health. Now for treats, there are MANY choices out there. You can find treats that are single ingredient (like freeze dried chicken or fish), or you can find treats that are more processed and can contain a lot of added ingredients. Not every processed treat is necessarily bad, but it's important to read the ingreadient list and make sure you are comfortable with what's in it. Treats should really only make up a small portion of your cat's diet.

Feeding Schedule

This section will most likely be a bit shorter than the others as feeding schedule is a pretty personal choice and can depend on your cat's indivdual needs and your lifestyle. Some people prefer to free feed their cat (dry food is usually the only option for this) which can be convenient and allows your cat to ear whenevr they want, but it can also lead to overeating and obesity if your cat doesn't have good portion control. If you choose to free feed, it's important to monitor your cat's weight and adjust the amount of food you are providing as needed. Another option is to feed your cat on a schedule, which can help with portion control and can also be a good way to bond with your cat. You can choose to feed your cat once a day, twice a day, or even more frequently if you have a kitten or a cat with special dietary needs. It all depends on what with your schedule as well as your cat's needs and preferences. Combination feeding (wet and dry) is also a common choice and can provide the benefits of both types of food. Ultimately, the most important thing is to find a feeding routine that works for you and your cat and make sure they are getting the nutrition they need to thrive.

Hydration & Water Intake

Cat's have a pretty low thirst drive, which is why it's so important to make sure they are getting enough water, especially if they are only eating dry food. Wet food can help with hydration since it contains a lot of moisture, but if your cat is eating dry food, it's important to provide them with plenty of fresh water. It's also a good idea to be mindful of the type of water bowl you are using, as some cats can be picky about this. A water bowl that is too small in diameter can cause whisker fatigue, which can make your cat less likely to drink. A wider, shallower bowl can help with this. I have dogs, as well as a cat, so I just use a large stainless steel bowl for both of them and it seems to work fine. I'm lucky enough that my cat will also beg for a water bowl refill if it's been sitting there for what he deems is too long. You can also try adding a water fountain to encourage your cat to drink more, as some cats prefer running water due to the movement. I will add, some cats may be as picky about their water and their food being close together, so if you notice your cat is not drinking as much water as they should be, try moving the water bowl to a different location away from their food bowl.
