Basics 101

It's important to understand the basics in cat care before you decide to bring one home. Think of this page as a "Day One Survival Guide." It will cover the essentials of cat care that won't be covered in the other pages. This includes things like creating a safe environment for your cat, understanding their body language, and how to properly introduce them to other pets in the household.

Creating a Safe Environment

If there is one task you want to take care of before bringing your furry friend home, it's creating a safe environment for them. Cats are naturally curious animals and they will explore EVERY single spot in your home. This means that you need to make sure that there are no dangerous items within their reach. By things, I mean items like toxic plants, wires that are plugged into the wall (cats love to chew on wires), and small objects that they could swallow. You should also make sure that all windows are closed and if applicable, that your balcony is either closed or cat-proofed if you decide to let them onto it. Lastly, and probably one of the most important, is to make sure that the room (and home in general) is free of any strong scents from cleaning products, air fresheners, candles, etc. You can get away with certian scents, but strong scents can be overwhelming for cats and cause them to feel stressed and anxious.

The Litter Box

The litter box is an essential part of your cat's daily routine. It should be large enough for your cat to comfortably turn around in and placed in a quite room. You should also to make sure to clean your litter box at least once a day to prevent any odors and to keep your cat happy. If you have multiple cats, it's recommended to have one litter box per cat, plus one extra. So if you have two cats, you should have three litter boxes. This will help prevent any territorial disputes.


If you plan on getting a kitten, you might need to start with something small and low to the ground, like a dispoble litter tray or a shallow storage container (make sure you clean regularly if you go with the storage container option). Some kittens might struggle with jumping into a regular litter box, but obviously this depends on the kitten. For litter, I would recommend starting with clumping litter that is unscented. Avoid clumping clay litter for kittens, as they can easily ingest it and it can cause serious health issues. I personally use the pine pellets from Tractor Supply with a sifting litter box and I feel that this has been the best decision. However, when I first got my cat I used the crystal litter. Big mistake. This litter tracks everywhere and just when you thought it was all clean, it's not. I did change my cats litter pretty young, but he took to it pretty well. Be mindful that changing litter for cats is like changing food, they can be very picky and might not take to the new litter right away. If you decide to change your cats litter, I would recommend doing it gradually by mixing the new litter with the old litter and slowly increasing the amount of new litter over time.


Another item I would recommend is the Litter Genie. If you have kids, you've probably heard of the Diaper Genie, well the Litter Genie is the cat version of that. It helps to contain the smell of the litter from the box and makes it easy to dispose of the waste. It's not a super big investment (about $30) and it will make your life so much easire. I would highly recommend it, especially if you have multiple cats or if your litter box is in a common area of the house.

Feline Body Language

Cats are big on boundaries and they will let you know when you are crossing them. It's important to understand your cat's body language so that you can respect their boundaries which can help build a strong bond between you and your cat. Lets start with the tail. A cat's tail can tell you a lot about their mood. If their tail is up and relaxed, they are happy and content. If it's puffed up, they are scared or agitated. If it's twitching, they are annoyed. However, twitching can also be a sign of excitement, so it's important to look at the rest of their body language to get a better understanding of how they are feeling. If their tail is wrapper around their body, they might be feeling anxious.


Now, let's talk about the ears. There are a couple different positions that a cat's ears can be in and each position can indicate a different mood. If their ears are "neutral" or forward and relaxed, then they are also most likely content and relaxed. It can also be a sign of curiosity if they are focused on something. If their ears are swiveling and rotating kind of like a satellite dish, they are most likely alert and trying to listen to something. Now, if their ears are flat against their head, they are most likely scared or agitated. Make sure to give them space if you see this, as they might be feeling threatened and could lash out if they feel like they are being cornered. The next one is always a funny one, the "airplane ears." This is when a cat's ears are flat against their head but they are also pointing outwars. It makes them look like they have airplane wings for ears, hence the name. This could mean a couple different things, they could be feeling angry, scared, or even in pain. In some cases, it could be a sign of intesne play or excitement. My cat does this when he gets a burst of energy and starts running around the house like a maniac. As always, it's good to check the rest of their body language to get a better understanding of what they might be feeling in that moment.


The last part of a cat's body language that I want to talk about is their eyes. Dialted pupils can be a sign of excitement or fear while constricted pupils can be a sign of aggression, intense focus, or just the lighting in the room. A hard stare can be a sign of either intense focus or dominance, similar to how dogs will stare at you when they want something. A slow blink (my favorite) is a sign of trust and affection. If your cat gives you a slow blink, it's like they are giving you a little kiss. You can even return the slow blink and show them that you trust and love them too. Keep in mind that not every cat is the same, some cats get overstimulated more quick than others, some are super chill. You don't have to take what I say and apply every little detail to your cat, just use it as a general guide to help you understand your cat better!
